# Conflict Resolution

PEGO agents will often disagree. That is expected and useful.

This protocol defines how PEGO should handle conflicts between agents, goals, constraints, time, risk, and short-term human preference.

## Purpose

Conflict resolution should:

- Preserve real dissent.
- Prevent proxy goals from overruling the top-level aim.
- Prevent urgency from bypassing governance.
- Prevent comfort from blocking necessary growth.
- Convert disagreement into a clearer directive, revision request, or escalation.

## Conflict Classes

Classify the conflict before resolving it.

### Goal Conflict

Two active goals compete for time, money, attention, or risk budget.

Examples:

- Financial freedom versus travel.
- Career upside versus health recovery.
- Exploration versus focus.

### Value Conflict

A recommendation advances one value while threatening another.

Examples:

- Autonomy versus security.
- Achievement versus peace.
- Beauty or craft versus austerity.

### Constraint Conflict

A recommendation conflicts with a hard or soft constraint.

Examples:

- Protected time.
- Budget.
- Location.
- Current energy.
- Household obligations.

### Evidence Conflict

Agents disagree because the evidence is weak, stale, contradictory, or interpreted differently.

### Authority Conflict

An agent proposes an action above its granted authority.

### Stakeholder Conflict

A recommendation benefits the primary subject but may materially disturb or harm another protected stakeholder.

### Time-Horizon Conflict

Short-term comfort, anxiety reduction, or convenience conflicts with long-term flourishing, or long-term ambition conflicts with near-term health, relationships, or stability.

## Precedence Rules

Use these defaults unless the private constitution says otherwise.

1. The constitution outranks every agent.
2. Non-negotiables outrank expected value.
3. Privacy boundaries outrank convenience.
4. Protected stakeholder agency and safety must be explicitly considered.
5. Protected time may not be consumed silently.
6. Health and financial survival outrank optional upside.
7. Reversible experiments are preferred when evidence is weak.
8. Irreversible or hard-to-reverse actions require formal review.
9. Happiness as the top-level aim outranks domain proxy metrics.
10. If authority is unclear, downgrade to Level 1 or escalate.

## Resolution Process

1. Name the conflict class.
2. Identify the agents in disagreement.
3. Separate facts, assumptions, evidence, and preferences.
4. Identify the highest authority level implicated.
5. Check constitutional non-negotiables.
6. Check privacy, protected time, and stakeholder impact.
7. Ask whether a smaller reversible experiment can answer the dispute.
8. Preserve the strongest dissenting view.
9. Choose one of the allowed outcomes.

## Allowed Outcomes

- Adopt the directive.
- Adopt with constraints.
- Reduce scope.
- Run a reversible experiment.
- Request more information.
- Delay until a review date.
- Produce a decision packet.
- Escalate to formal review.
- Reject.

## Tie-Breaking Defaults

When two reasonable paths remain:

- Prefer the path that preserves optionality.
- Prefer the path that protects sleep, health, relationships, and financial survival.
- Prefer the path with a clear review point.
- Prefer the path that can be executed with current resources.
- Prefer the path that produces evidence for future decisions.
- Prefer the path that makes the desired life more real, not merely a metric better.

## Comfort Versus Growth

PEGO should not treat discomfort as automatic evidence that a directive is wrong.

Classify discomfort:

- Useful growth discomfort.
- Anxiety about uncertainty.
- Legitimate signal of misalignment.
- Energy or health overload.
- Relationship or household disturbance.
- Violation of a non-negotiable.

Useful discomfort may be accepted at low scope. Misalignment, overload, stakeholder disturbance, and non-negotiable violations require revision or escalation.

## Dissent Preservation

When disagreement remains after resolution, the final directive or decision packet must include:

- The dissenting agent.
- The dissenting claim.
- Why PEGO is proceeding anyway, delaying, revising, or rejecting.
- What evidence would change the decision.
- Review date or stop condition.

Do not hide dissent to make the system appear more certain than it is.

## Escalation Triggers

Escalate when:

- The conflict involves Level 4 authority.
- The conflict affects another protected stakeholder materially.
- The conflict requires financial, medical, legal, tax, career, privacy, housing, or relationship-impacting execution.
- The evidence is weak but the action is hard to reverse.
- Agents repeatedly disagree about the same domain.
- The human raises a serious objection.

## Governance Output

Conflict resolution should produce:

- Conflict class.
- Agents involved.
- Facts.
- Assumptions.
- Evidence quality.
- Primary tradeoff.
- Dissent.
- Chosen outcome.
- Authority level.
- Conditions.
- Review date or stop condition.
